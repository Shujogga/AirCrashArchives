Air France Flight 447 – What Really Happened

Date of Incident: June 1, 2009

Location: Mid-Atlantic Ocean between Brazil and Senegal

🧭 Flight Summary

Air France Flight 447 was an overnight flight from Rio de Janeiro to Paris. The Airbus A330 disappeared over the Atlantic Ocean with 228 people onboard. It took years of investigation to uncover the sequence of failures that led to one of aviation’s most puzzling accidents.

✈️ Aircraft Details

Flight Number: AF447

Aircraft Type: Airbus A330-203

Registration: F-GZCP

Departure: Rio de Janeiro-Galeão International (GIG)

Destination: Paris Charles de Gaulle (CDG)

Fatalities: All 228 onboard

Impact Time: Approx. 02:14 UTC

🔍 Weather and Technical Factors

The aircraft entered a zone of severe turbulence due to towering thunderstorms in the Intertropical Convergence Zone. Ice crystals obstructed all three pitot tubes, causing airspeed data to become unreliable. This led to the autopilot disengaging and manual control being handed to the crew under challenging conditions.

📆 Timeline of Events

  • 01:35 UTC – Last radio contact with Brazilian ATC
  • 01:51 UTC – Entered turbulence and lost airspeed readings
  • 02:10 UTC – Aircraft stalled at high altitude due to incorrect pitch inputs
  • 02:14 UTC – Impact with the Atlantic Ocean
  • 06:00 UTC – Official confirmation of loss by French authorities

🎙️ Cockpit Voice & Flight Data

“We’ve lost the speed readings.” — First Officer
“What’s happening? I don’t understand what's happening.” — Captain (seconds before impact)

The CVR and FDR were recovered in 2011 after extensive underwater searches. Investigators concluded that a stall induced by incorrect pilot response went unresolved for over three minutes.

⚙️ Aircraft Systems & Failures

  • Pitot tube icing led to unreliable airspeed
  • Autopilot disengaged automatically due to data loss
  • Manual flight entered high-altitude stall
  • Flight crew did not recognize stall symptoms
  • Recovery actions were delayed and ineffective

🛡️ Investigative Outcome

  • BEA Final Report released in July 2012
  • Highlighted CRM (Crew Resource Management) shortcomings
  • Pitot tube manufacturer changed materials and design
  • Training modified to better detect and recover from high-altitude stalls
